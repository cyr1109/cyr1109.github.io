Research



Digitization, Privacy, and Intellectual Property:

I study what influences and motivates choices in information markets. I am especially interested in data markets where individuals supply personal data to firms. I am also interested in the interaction of rights assignments and information-sharing behavior. My research method primarily uses randomized, controlled experiments to elicit decisions over real outcomes.

Privacy-seeking behavior in the personal data market online experiment

[job market paper]

Internet users regularly choose to generate and share personal data in return for goods and services. Firms are seeking digital strategies for exploiting personal data assets. Average consumers are excluded from this secondary data market.

An online experiment with 1,188 participants was conducted to elicit individuals' willingness-to-share their personally-identifiable, user-generated, psychometric data with others. I predicted and found that individuals behaved more privately in response to greater awareness of a data recipient's secondary market exploitation abilities (or demanded greater benefits in exchange for privacy-losses). I also found that privacy-seeking behavior was less responsive to increased exposure (to more data recipients) than information provisions about secondary data markets.

Note: An early version of this project was presented at the 2019 Consortium on Competitiveness and Cooperation (CCC) under the title "The Value of Privacy."
The effects of rights assignments on licensing ideas lab + online experiment

Licensing agreements are popular for trading ideas without exchanging the ownership rights to those ideas, and their royalty amounts are often negotiated between the licensors and licensees. These decisions may be subject to biases and heuristics since the valuation of ideas is inherently difficult and intellectual property rights are abstract.

Using three experimental studies (N = {167, 272, 283}), I measured individualsâ€™ royalty amounts they were willing-to-accept (or pay) to license potential solutions to a logic problem. I predicted and found disparities in acceptable royalty amounts between licensors and licensees of ideas, even in the absence of information asymmetries (i.e., between non-creator owners and non-owners). I also found that valuation gaps for higher quality ideas were tempered by providing licensors and licensees with third-party expert assessments of an idea's potential to succeed; however, licensors persisted in inflating their royalty amounts (relative to licensees) for low quality ideas.
